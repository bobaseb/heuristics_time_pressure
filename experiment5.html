Learning and Decision Making Psychology Study (Developing Countries): Practice Phase

In this study, you will learn to use a rule that predicts which developing country will increase their Gross Domestic Product (GDP) more compared to another developing country based on statistics of their economy gathered in 2013. We want you to learn how to use a very specific rule. The rule does quite well so we are more interested in you learning the rule than actually trying to learn the predictive value of each statistic directly. For each statistic, you will be told whether the country did well or poorly on that measure. All information is taken from reliable sources (World Bank, IMF, etc.), though we hide country names so that you can't rely on any other information you might know about a given country in the developing world. On each trial, you will be shown the stats for two countries and asked what the rule would predict. In other words, if the rule would predict that Country A increased their GDP more than Country B or vice versa. Your performance on this part of the study will only evaluate how well you use the rule that will be explained on the next screen.

Statistics will be qualified by the positive terms "greater", "higher", "larger", "stronger", "superior", "more stable" and "better". And the corresponding negative terms are "lesser", "lower", "smaller", "weaker", "inferior", "less stable" and "worse". To know which statistic is better for which country you will have to compare terms for that statistic. For example, if interest rates were "better" for Country A and "worse" for Country B, then Country A would be better off on that statistic. Statistics can also be tied between countries, so for the latter example you would see "equal" for Country A and "equal" for Country B in which case the countries have equal performance for that statistic. Just to be clear, positive terms always predict better GDP increase for that country. Based on these stats, if you think the rule predicts Country A will increase their GDP more than Country B, press A for Country A. Otherwise, press B for Country B.

After responding, you will be told whether the rule would make that prediction or not.

Please try to keep your index fingers resting on the A and B keys at all times.

The entire experiment should last around 40 minutes.

Please read through the whole list of the seven stats.
During the experiment, when one developing country has a better value of the stat, it will show
a positive term; "greater", "higher", "larger", "stronger", "superior", "more stable" or "better".
Positive terms ALWAYS predict better GDP performance for that country.
If the statistic shows the word "equal" for both countries then the countries are tied for that statistic.
The purpose of the experiment is that you learn to recognize the important stats.
You can see the ranking of the stats from most to least important on the right.
To do this you should use the following rule in your judgments:

Rule:

If the best statistic is not tied between countries, then answer in favor of the country that does better on that statistic.

Otherwise, if the best statistic is tied for both countries (both countries say "equal") then look at the next best statistic on the list and answer in favor of that country and so on.

In other words, go through the list of ranked stats one by one until you find one that is not tied and answer accordingly.
